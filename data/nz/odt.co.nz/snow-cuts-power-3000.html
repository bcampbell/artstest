Snow cuts power to 3000

Home » News » Regions
By Olivia Caldwell on Thu, 20 Jun 2013
News: Dunedin | The Regions: Otago | Weather
Previous || > Next
 

About 3000 people in the Mackenzie district are without power this morning as the area experiences snowfalls of about half a metre.

Heavy snow has already fallen across the Mackenzie today, and although Waitaki will not feel the full force of the expected snowstorm until tonight, some roads south of the Waitaki River are effected by surface flooding.

Mackenzie Civil Defence emergency management officer Ray Gardner said there was about 150mm of snow on the ground in Fairlie, but the situation around the rest of the district was varied.

"Around the district there is about half a metre at Tekapo, the same at Mt Cook, but Twizel has only got an inch. Generally, reports from farmers vary between about 400mm and 600mm around the district here, depending where they are, but nothing at this stage that anyone is concerned about.''

There had been "plenty of warning'' before the snow and most people had been well prepared, he said.

However, he added that the Fairlie Basin area between the substation at Albury and Fairlie lost power about 4am this morning.

He said there was about 3000 people in the affected area, and added that a fault was suspected as the cause.

"At this stage snow ploughs are out on the State Highways, and the snow plough has been around the township here in Fairlie and they are working there way out to rural areas now.''

In North Otago, snow was being blamed for two accidents today, Senior Constable Craig Bennett, from Kurow, said.

"I 've had one vehicle crash with three occupants. One male has been taken to Oamaru and then through to Dunedin Hospital with a complicated fracture of the hand with the bone protruding.

''And then I've had a farmer who was out feeding his sheep and an embankment in the snow has given way and pushed his vehicle into the middle of the Hakataramea River. So he had a cold wake-up and we had to get a tractor to extract him.''

He added that conditions around Kurow were "very slushy'' and motorists should drive slowly and to conditions.  

State Highway 8 between Fairlie and Twizel, and between Pukaki and Mt Cook is closed.

Waitaki Civil Defence emergency services manager Chris Raine said there was also a covering of snow on State Highway 83 from Duntroon westwards, and there was flooding on State Highway 1, south of Oamaru at Waianakarua and Shag Point.

"There may be some fresh (road) closures due to either rain or snow at some point today.''

The Mackenzie had received the bulk of snow so far, but the main southerly front would hit North Otago later tonight, he said.

"So tomorrow morning we should probably see snow lower down.''

The Maniototo has borne the brunt of the wintry blast in Central Otago, with reports of 30-40cm of snow in Naseby, 20cm in Ranfurly, 25cm through the Ida Valley and snow continuing to fall this morning.

All Central Otago District Council roads in Ranfurly, Naseby and the top end of the Ida Valley are closed and roading contractors are focusing on clearing streets in the two towns as well as clearing roads in the Ida Valley.

Motorists are advised to postpone travelling as it is still snowing.  

Rest of Otago spared

The rest of Otago appears to have been spared the worst of the polar blast that is working its way up the South Island .

While snow has closed highways and stranded motorists in some areas, the expected heavy falls have yet to eventuate in many places.

Residents in at least one community - Millers Flat - were without power earlier but it has now been restored.

Snow began settling on Dunedin's hill suburbs about 8pm but has now disappeared. Police had no reports of accidents in the city, and said motorists appeared to be heeding warnings to stay at home.

Road closures around the city this morning remain those resulting from the rain earlier in the week (check Dunedin road conditions here).

Further afield, there is a raft of closures on South Island highways, including SH85 Omakau to Palmerston, SH87 Kyeburn to Outram, SH8 Lindis Pass, SH6; 97 Kingston to Lumsden; Five Rivers to Mossburn (check highway conditions here).

Radio New Zealand this morning is reporting heavy snow in the Tekapo area, and says there have been power outages in inland Canterbury and the Mackenzie Basin.

MetService earlier upgraded its heavy snow warnings to include Otago and Southland, and says rain was expected to turn to snow above 200m and lower to sea level last night.

''This is a significant event which is expected to persist through until Friday morning,'' the forecast said.

The Dunedin Airport website lists a couple of flight cancellations, while the Queenstown Airport site shows at least one cancellation and some delays.

Snow in Southland left some motorists stranded yesterday evening.

Senior Sergeant Dave Raynes, of Invercargill, said 20 vehicles were stranded on the Jollies Hill Pass on State Highway 6, between Kingston and Lumsden. A further six vehicles had slid off the road.

As the snow fell it was freezing and contractors had to use a grit truck and grader to clear the road and rescue the motorists, he said.

Stocking up

Milk, bread and firewood were in high demand across Otago yesterday as residents prepared for the cold snap.

Social support organisations also took precautions by stocking up their cupboards and organising alternative transport in case of road closures before the weekend.

Yesterday, supermarkets had their bread trays emptied and car parks filled, and the queues were continuous.

Pak'n Save Dunedin co-owner William McDonald said while yesterday's influx was noticeable, he had witnessed the same thing in past years when the MetService had forecast snow.

''It's busy, but this is nothing new. It's just people being aware of the weather and getting prepared.''

Extra stock from the store's Christchurch distribution centre had been ordered in case of a road closure. An extra delivery for the frozen food section had also been organised in readiness for today, freezer assistant Colin Tocher said.

Bradford resident and mother of four Phillippa Scott attempted to beat the rush by filling her trolley yesterday, but was joined by queues of people with the same idea. She said the price of her weekly shop was likely to be twice its usual size as she prepared for the weather.

Social organisations such as Dunedin's Salvation Army, the Methodist Mission, Age Concern and the St Barnabas Trust had taken weather precautions. Both St Barnabas Trust and Age Concern Otago's meals on wheels organisations yesterday contacted the Land Rover Owners Club to transport their meals throughout Dunedin, if necessary.

St Barnabas Trust's freezers were stocked with frozen meals to last a week, co-ordinator Lynette Keith said.

More staff had been rostered on and gas ovens were prepared in case of a power failure.

''We could feed 200 people for two weeks with the food we have here already.''

Southern District Health Board operations manager Megan Boivin said no health services would be cancelled or scaled down until the extent of any snowfall was known.

People with hospital appointments today or tomorrow should listen to the radio for updates, she said.

If necessary, the board would activate its emergency operations centre, where calls from patients would be directed in the event of disruption.

Flights around the country were affected by the weather conditions, including three flights between Christchurch and Queenstown.
