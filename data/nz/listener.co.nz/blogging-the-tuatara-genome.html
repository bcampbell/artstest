Blogging the tuatara genome

A major new project to sequence and undestand the DNA of NZ's best looking native animal.

By Toby Manhire In Science, The Internaut

Print Share
  • Tweet
20th June, 2013
  • Biology
  • Genetics
  • Tuatara
Leave a Comment

A tuatara, with genes. Photo: Andrew Warner

The most handsome and enduring inhabitants of New Zealand, they are sometimes described as “living dinosaurs”.

In fact, writes biologist and science blogger David Winter, “tuatara are much more interesting that that”.

In evolutionary terms, dinosaurs are near cousins of birds. “The tuatara, on the other hand, are the only living members of a lineage that separated from other reptiles more than 200 million years ago.”

For that reason, Winter and colleagues have embarked on the Tuatara Genome Project, a major collaboration involving local groups, iwi and international partners.

For scientists, it is a “tantalising prospect”. The cost in time and money is easily worthwhile, says Winter in an introductory post, because “we know a tuatara genome sequence will provide a unique insight into how evolution works, and serve as a valuable resource to other scientists”.

And Winter is blogging the enterprise at New Zealand’s excellent SciBlogs site, promising “an insight into how a genome sequence is produced”.

With luck it might help ensure that the tuatara, an endangered species, endures: genetic data can be used by conservationists to “manage the species in a way that increases its chance of survival”, he says.
